Questions

The questions you're probably asking.

Fair question — and the right one. The honest answer is: you shouldn't trust any prediction tool on faith alone. So here's what we do instead. For founding partner brands, we run a retroactive backtest first: we process your last season's top SKUs through the model and show you how our forecast would have compared to your actual sell-through. You see the accuracy before you use it on a live buy. If the signal isn't useful, you don't proceed. The bar isn't perfection — it's whether this gives you more signal than Instagram polls and a WGSN report. We think it does. We let the backtest make that case.

EDITED is one of the best tools on the market for understanding what sold across your competitive set last season. WGSN is excellent for macro trend direction. Neither can tell you whether your specific new product concept will sell next season — because that data doesn't exist yet. We're not replacing either tool. We sit in the one gap they structurally cannot fill: the zero-data, new-product, pre-production decision. WGSN gives you category direction. EDITED tells you what the market did. Drawing Board tells you the probabilistic demand distribution for your new product before you commit — in units, by price point, by region. Those are additive tools. We're the layer that was missing.

We hear this often — and the irony isn't lost on us. The season that makes a tool like this most valuable is the one that makes the budget tightest. So here's what we offer founding partner brands: a pilot sprint at $4,500 — less than what most boutique research firms charge for a single focus group, and a fraction of a typical writedown. If the output doesn't meaningfully improve your buy decision, you stop. If it does, you have a defensible ROI case for every subsequent sprint. The risk of trying is $4,500. The risk of not trying is another season of buying blind.

Fundamentally different — not just faster. Surveys and synthetic audience tools ask people what they think they'd buy. People are unreliable predictors of their own future behavior, especially in a no-stakes context. Drawing Board doesn't simulate consumers. It's a probabilistic demand model trained on real transaction data and product attributes — it finds products with analogous attributes in its training data and forecasts what demand looks like for yours. The output isn't "consumers would consider purchasing this." It's "based on what actually sold for comparable products, here is your demand distribution at this price point, in this region, for this category." That's a different class of prediction.
